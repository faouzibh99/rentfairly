Rent Split Calculator: Why Equal Split Is Almost Always Unfair

Your roommate has a private en-suite bathroom, a huge closet, and south-facing windows. You have a shoebox room next to the street. Equal rent? That's not fair — that's lazy math.

The Equal Split Trap

Equal splits feel democratic. Divide the rent by the number of people and everyone pays the same — simple, no arguments. But "no arguments" isn't the same as "fair." Equal splits just delay the resentment.

Research on roommate disputes consistently shows that money — specifically rent and bills — is the #1 cause of roommate conflicts. And most of those conflicts stem from someone feeling they're overpaying for what they get.

💡 Real example: $3,000/month apartment, 2 roommates. Equal split = $1,500 each. But Room A is 250 sq ft with a private bathroom. Room B is 130 sq ft with a shared bathroom. Fair split? Roughly $1,820 and $1,180. The equal split costs the person in Room B an extra $320 per month — or $3,840 per year.

What Makes a Room Valuable?

Think of it like a mini real estate market inside your apartment. Each room has a market value based on its features:

  • Square footage — the most objective measure of space
  • Bathroom access — private bath adds significant value
  • Light quality — sunny rooms command premiums in the rental market
  • Noise exposure — street-facing rooms are worth less
  • Storage — closets, built-ins, and shelving add livability
  • Extras — balcony access, better views, proximity to kitchen

The Algorithm Behind Fair Splits

Our calculator uses a weighted scoring system that mirrors how the rental market actually prices rooms:

  • Room size → 50% weight
  • Natural light → 15% weight
  • Noise level → 15% weight
  • Private bathroom → 12% weight
  • Closet space → 5% weight
  • Extra perks → 3% weight

Each room gets a score. That score as a percentage of the total determines what share of rent is fair.

How to Convince a Roommate the Equal Split Isn't Fair

The key is to remove the personal element. Don't say "I think I should pay less." Instead, share a link to the calculation and let the algorithm do the talking. When a neutral tool produces the number, it's harder to argue against.

If your roommate still resists, suggest each of you scores the rooms independently. Average the scores. It's hard to argue with a process you both participated in.
