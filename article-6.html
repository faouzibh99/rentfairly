My Roommate Won't Pay Rent — What Can I Do?

One of the most stressful situations in shared living: your roommate is not paying rent and you are on the hook. Here is exactly what to do.

Advertisement

Step 1: Have a Direct Conversation First

Before anything else, talk to your roommate directly. Sometimes non-payment is a miscommunication or a temporary financial hardship. Approach calmly: "I noticed the rent has not been paid — is everything okay?" goes further than threats.

Step 2: Check Your Lease Structure

This is critical. Are you on a joint lease or do you each have a separate lease?

  • Joint lease — you are both legally responsible for the full rent. If your roommate does not pay, the landlord can come after you for their share.
  • Separate leases — your roommate's non-payment is between them and the landlord, not your legal problem.

Step 3: Document Everything

Send a written message (text or email) referencing the unpaid amount and the due date. This creates a paper trail. Keep records of all communications.

Step 4: Give a Written Deadline

Give your roommate a specific deadline in writing: "Please pay your share of $X by [date]." Be firm but professional.

Step 5: Contact Your Landlord

If you are on a joint lease and at risk of eviction, inform your landlord of the situation. They may have options — some landlords will pursue the non-paying tenant directly.

Step 6: Small Claims Court

If your roommate has left you financially damaged — you paid their rent to avoid eviction — you can sue them in small claims court. This is simpler than most people think and does not require a lawyer.

Prevention is far better than cure. A written roommate agreement signed before moving in gives you legal standing if you ever need to pursue this route.
