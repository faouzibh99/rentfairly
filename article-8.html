How to Split Rent When Roommates Have Different Incomes

One roommate earns twice what the other does. Should they pay more rent? It is one of the most debated questions in shared living — and there is no single right answer.

Two Philosophies of Fair Rent

Philosophy 1: Room-Based Fairness

You pay for what you get. The bigger, nicer room costs more — regardless of who lives in it. This is the most common approach. It treats the apartment like a market: the room has a value, and the occupant pays that value.

Philosophy 2: Income-Based Fairness

You pay what you can afford. Higher earners contribute more so that everyone can live together without financial strain on the lower earner. More common among couples or very close friends.

How to Calculate an Income-Based Split

  1. Add both incomes together: $4,000 + $6,000 = $10,000 total
  2. Calculate each person's share of total income: 40% and 60%
  3. Apply those percentages to the rent: $2,000 total → $800 and $1,200
Income-based splits require full financial transparency between roommates. Both parties must be comfortable sharing their income figures — which many people are not, especially with people they are not close to.

Which Method Should You Use?

  • Close friends or couples — income-based can work well with open communication
  • New or casual roommates — room-based is cleaner, less personal, and easier to agree on
  • Mixed situations — use room-based as the base, then adjust slightly if both agree

The Hybrid Approach

Some roommates use a combination: start with a room-based fair split, then if the lower-earning roommate genuinely cannot afford their share, the higher earner temporarily covers more — documented as a loan to be repaid when circumstances improve.
